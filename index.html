What is UWU?
uwu is an emoticon that can be pronounced by the way it is spelled (“OO-WOO”). It is an expression of a smiley face that denotes cuteness and can largely be found on the internet, characterized by au, w, and a u placed next to each other to cutesify a sentence. The expression itself is meant to be: ‘happy in a smug kind of way’.

uwu does not stand for anything, but can be used to help express a particularly cutesy and/or childish way of talking. Like how uwu can express happy emotions, it can also express a condescending tone, an ironic cool demeanor, or be used as conversation filler in text when someone has nothing to say back (similar to how we use lol and lmfao today).
However, that in itself is just how uwu is used in its text format. uwu is very, very versatile in text and is used frequently in meme culture. From social media to texting to online aesthetics, uwu is everywhere.
Where is UWU used?

uwu largely lives on the internet, but I have no doubt that there are people in real life who use uwu unironically and probably say it out loud lol. uwu is used frequently in the furry community as well, l argely due to their beleif that the ‘w’ in uwu shares a likeness to an anthropormorphic snout.
There is no exact way to use uwu. Like a lot of internet culture, phrases and words taken from there have largely different meanings today compared to how they were used in the past. The uwus of back then were probably not used in the same way like the uwus of today. And remember, remember, uwu is a tool that people use to help express themselves. Therefore, the meaning of uwu will change depending on who is using it and what they are using it for. uwu, ultimately, is a tool for self-expression, and what people choose to do with such a tool is up to them.

What is the origin
of UWU?


No one is sure about the exact origins of uwu. Some say it originated in online chatrooms, with its first appearance being in a Yu-Gi-Oh fanfiction, Genie of the Puzzle, published by user DaakuKitsune on October 14th, 2005. In the author's notes before the story, the author states

“Again, feel free to throw squids and fish at me. UwU I deserve it, you know”.


Others say it originated from Japanese anime culture, where it was influenced by the ‘kawaii’ face made by anime characters - which, considering the likeness, is probably true.
But one thing is for certain, like many internet trends, once tumblr picked it up, uwu’s destiny was set in stone.
Problematic UWU?


Of course, there are downsides to every internet trend. uwu is not exempt from being associated with some questionable behaviors, no matter how cute. People on the internet raised concerns that usage of uwu in addition to using ‘kawaii’ mannerisms would be weaponized by older men looking to catfish and take advantage of young people on the internet by using this to seem more approachable and younger, posing a danger to others.
The aesthetic, uwu girl trend, relies on the image of the hyper-feminine gamer girl persona. As stated by Ashley Bardhan in Input, who has interviewed several female discord gamers who adopt the uwu girl aesthetic as a way to gain access into a field that is highly male dominated, “uwu girl” “is a girl who brings the emoticon to life through her aesthetic and her demeanor, which tends to be sweetly childlike, but also suggestive, drawing on the sexualization of (usually young) anime girls. The aesthetic involves lots and lots of pink and cutesifying of self through makeup that prioritizes unnaturally big eyes and fake freckles, invoking cartoon characters like Sailor Moon and the concept of the hyperfeminine gamer girl.
Not to be confused with “softgirls,” who also like pink but don’t play video games (think queen of doing that “sexy baby” thing, Ariana Grande), or “egirls,” who play video games and like anime, but prefer black fishnets and chokers to an uwu girl’s pink miniskirt.” While it is okay to embody the fashion of an aesthetic, these traits of childlike behavior associated with anime girls that these people are basing their personas off of to an audience who is most likely going to going fetishize them are also demeaning and play off of, no matter how unintentional, the submissive Asian woman trope - who must be hyperfeminine, youthful and childlike, dependent, but also unintentionally sexy. This is not meant to imply that uwu is the sole cause of this, nor is it meant to imply that uwu is something that came directly from Japanese culture - as the origins of uwu are rather blurry and honestly probably moreso a constantly changing product of the internet - depending on the social media landscape - but, it does play a role in many weeb circles, is used in the e-girl trend, and contributes to the overall ‘anime girl aesthetic’ - which originated from Japanese anime culture - which contributes to the commodification of such traits, especially when the uwu and/or E-girl persona is being performed by non-Asian people. And, it is literally called the ‘uwu girl aesthetic’.
Also, differing from the UWU girls of discord, the E-girls of tiktok take their look from Japanese Harajuku fashion, which is based on gothic-influenced and cosplay-style clothing. Many individuals believe that this fashion emerged on tiktok, which, it did not.
